§2 Geometry & derived dimensions
Synchronous speed
N_s = 60 · f / p
Theoretical no-load speed set by supply frequency and pole count. Doubling p halves N_s. Actual operating speed includes 4% slip: N_op = N_s × 0.96.
rpm
Pole pitch (arc length on rotor surface)
τ = π · D_r / (2 · p)
Physical distance between adjacent pole centres on the rotor. Used to compute flux per pole. Larger rotor → longer pitch → more flux linkage.
m
Mean turn length (winding)
L_mean = π(r_r + δ + 0.01) + 2·L_m + 0.04
Approximate path length of one conductor turn: two straight slot sides (2·L_m) plus two end-winding semicircles. The 0.01 and 0.04 m constants account for end-winding overhang.
m
Slot area (approximate)
A_slot = π·((D_s−D_r−2δ)/4)² · (r_inner · 2π / Q) · 0.65
The usable cross-section of one slot, estimated as a wedge-shaped region. Factor 0.65 accounts for tooth wedges and insulation area that conductors cannot occupy.
mm²
§3 Magnetic circuit
Peak air-gap flux density (from voltage)
B_peak = V_s / (4.44 · f · N · p · τ · L_m) ≤ 1.70 T
Transformer EMF equation rearranged for B. The 4.44 = 2√2 · π/2 (form factor for sinusoidal flux). Clamped at 1.70 T — silicon steel saturates ~1.6–1.8 T. Exceeding this triggers the "Flux saturation" alert.
T (tesla)
Effective air-gap flux density
B_air = B_peak · 0.85
The 0.85 leakage factor accounts for flux that crosses the air gap but doesn't link the stator conductors (slot leakage, end leakage, fringing). Used in all torque and iron-loss calculations.
T
Skew factor
k_sk = cos(α / 2) [α in radians]
Skewing slots or magnets by angle α reduces cogging torque ripple but also reduces the fundamental EMF. At α = 10° → k_sk ≈ 0.996. At α = 30° → k_sk ≈ 0.966. Directly multiplies Kt.
dimensionless
§4 Winding resistance
Wire cross-sectional area
A_w = π · (d_w / 2)²
Bare wire area in m² (d_w entered in mm, converted). Doubling wire diameter quadruples A_w, quartering resistance and copper loss at fixed current.
m²
Phase resistance (one phase, all N turns)
R_phase = ρ · N · L_mean / A_w
Ohm's Law for a single conductor of total length N·L_mean and cross section A_w. ρ = 1.72×10⁻⁸ Ω·m for annealed copper at 20°C. Resistance scales linearly with N and L_mean, inversely with A_w.
Ω
Slot fill factor
k_fill = N · A_w · 10⁶ / A_slot
Ratio of copper to available slot area. Alert thresholds: yellow ≥ 0.55, red ≥ 0.70. Practical machine-wound motors rarely exceed 0.65; hand-wound hairpin can reach 0.75.
dimensionless (0–1)
Copper mass
m_Cu = N · Q · L_mean · A_w · 8960 · 1000
Volume of all conductors × density of copper (8960 kg/m³) × 1000 g/kg. A useful cost and weight proxy. Proportional to N, Q, and A_w.
grams
§5 Torque constant & back-EMF
Torque constant K_t (SI — in consistent units)
K_t = (3 · p · B_air · r_r · L_m · N · 0.95) / π · k_sk
Lorentz force law integrated over all conductors in the air gap. The 0.95 winding distribution factor accounts for the fact that conductors are spread across slots, not concentrated at one point. K_e = K_t in SI units (this is the back-EMF constant in V·s/rad).
N·m/A
Back-EMF (at operating speed)
E = K_e · ω = K_t · (N_op · 2π / 60)
Voltage generated by rotor spinning in the stator field. When E ≥ V_applied, the motor cannot accelerate further — triggers the "Back-EMF ≥ supply" alert. Scales linearly with speed and K_t.
V
Operating torque
T = K_t · I
Torque is strictly proportional to current for a BLDC/ECM motor with constant flux. In Const. CFM / Const. Speed modes the displayed torque is the load torque required to hold that setpoint.
N·m
T-N curve (family of curves for each design)
T(n) = K_t · I_r · (1 − (n/N_s)^1.8) · k_sk
An empirical curve shape: torque falls as speed rises due to increasing back-EMF reducing available current. Exponent 1.8 gives a shape between the ideal linear (1.0) and the induction-motor breakdown-slip curve (2.0).
N·m vs rpm
§6 Power & losses
Angular velocity
ω = N_op · 2π / 60
Converts rpm to rad/s for SI power and EMF calculations.
rad/s
Shaft power output
P_shaft = T · ω
Mechanical power delivered to the load (fan). For a centrifugal fan, this equals Q_fan × ΔP_fan / η_fan. The simulation uses this as the output of the motor model.
W
Copper (I²R) loss — all three phases
P_Cu = 3 · I² · (R_phase / 3) = I² · R_phase
Joule heating in the windings. Scales with I² — doubling current quadruples this loss. Dominant at low speed / high torque. Drives temperature rise and is the primary reason motors overheat at low-speed, high-load conditions.
W
Iron (core) loss — eddy currents & hysteresis
P_Fe = 0.003 · B_air² · f · D_s² · L_m · 7650 · 50
Empirical Steinmetz-type formula. 7650 kg/m³ is silicon-steel density; 50 is a normalisation constant. Scales with B² (so strongly with voltage) and linearly with frequency. Dominant at high speed and high flux.
W
Electrical input power (real)
P_in = V · I · PF
Real (active) power drawn from the supply. The power factor accounts for reactive current in motor inductance. Apparent power = V·I (VA). Reactive power Q = V·I·√(1−PF²).
W
Overall motor efficiency
η = P_shaft / P_in × 100%
Simple ratio. Losses not directly modelled: mechanical (bearing friction, windage). Alert: η < 50% = red, < 70% = amber. ECM motors in HVAC typically achieve 85–92% at rated point.
%
§7 Fan load & thermal
Fan CFM — cube law (Affinity Law)
CFM = 800 · (N_op / N_rated)³
Fan affinity law: airflow scales linearly with speed; pressure with speed²; power with speed³. Baselined at 800 CFM at rated speed. Halving speed cuts airflow to 1/2 but power to 1/8. This is why variable-speed HVAC fans are dramatically more efficient at part load.
ft³/min
Fan power vs speed (shaft power curve)
P_fan ∝ N³ → P_shaft curve shape
The shaft power chart shows T(n)·ω. For a purely cube-law fan load, P ∝ n³. For the motor simulation, the actual T·ω at each speed point is plotted — giving the characteristic rising power curve that peaks near stall (maximum torque) region.
W
Temperature rise (simplified)
ΔT = P_Cu / (10 · A_surface)
P_Cu dissipated through the stator outer surface. A_surface ≈ π·D_s·L_s. The constant 10 W/m²·K is a very conservative natural-convection heat transfer coefficient. Real motors use forced airflow (30–50 W/m²·K) giving lower ΔT. Alert: ΔT > 50°C = amber, > 80°C = red.
°C above ambient
Iron loss vs speed (scaling)
P_Fe(n) = P_Fe_rated · (n / N_rated)^1.5
Iron losses increase with speed (eddy current losses scale with frequency²; hysteresis with frequency). The 1.5 exponent is a compromise between pure eddy (f²) and pure hysteresis (f¹) dominance.
W
§8 Voltage sensitivity (electrical tab)
Speed derating under voltage sag
N(V) = N_rated · (V / V_s) · 0.96
For a BLDC/ECM with closed-loop speed control, the controller compensates up to the point where V_applied limits peak current or back-EMF. In open-loop approximation used here: speed scales linearly with voltage.
rpm
Torque vs applied voltage
T(V) = K_t · I · (V / V_s)
As voltage drops, the effective flux linkage available to produce torque also drops (assuming constant current from the drive). This models the case where the drive cannot boost current to compensate.
N·m
Input power vs voltage (at fixed I, PF)
P_in(V) = V · I · PF
Linear with voltage at constant current and PF. The slope of this line is I·PF.
W
Operating speed from voltage ratio
V_ratio = V / V_s    →    N_op = N_rated · V_ratio
Voltage ratio used throughout. If V < 0.85·V_s, the "Voltage sag >15%" alert fires — at this level, most AC drives will begin current-limiting or fault.
dimensionless

§10 Design rules of thumb for HVAC ECM motors
Air gap sizing
δ ≈ 0.005 to 0.012 × D_r
For BLDC permanent magnet rotors, the air gap can be smaller than induction motors (magnets provide flux regardless of gap). Smaller gap → better flux linkage but tighter mechanical tolerances and higher cogging.
Slot fill target
k_fill : 0.40 (auto) → 0.55–0.65 (hand-wound)
Automatic winding machines achieve 0.35–0.45. Careful hand-winding or hairpin conductors can reach 0.65–0.72. Beyond 0.70 is practically impossible without conductor deformation.
D/L ratio
D_s / L : 0.8–2.5 (pancake → long)
Fan motors tend to be "pancake" shaped (high D/L ≈ 2–3) to fit inside the fan hub. Shaft-mounted motors can be long (D/L ≈ 0.5–1.0) for better heat dissipation along the shaft.
Slot / pole ratio
Q / (2p) : 1.5, 2, or 3 preferred
12 slots / 4 poles (ratio 3) and 9 slots / 6 poles (ratio 1.5) are very common for BLDC because they give low cogging torque and balanced three-phase windings with fractional pitch.
Stator / rotor diameter ratio
D_r / D_s : 0.55–0.70
Too small a rotor → long flux path in teeth, high iron loss. Too large → inadequate slot depth, low fill, high leakage. Values around 0.60–0.65 are typical for 3-phase HVAC motors.
B_air operating point
B_air ≈ 0.7–1.1 T (BLDC with NdFeB magnets)
Higher B → more torque per unit volume, but increases iron loss (B² dependence) and risks saturation. NdFeB magnets allow higher B than ferrite; silicon steel saturates at ~1.6–1.8 T.
Note on model approximations: The lab uses a lumped-parameter, steady-state model. It does not model: magnetic saturation curves (only a hard clamp at 1.70 T), harmonics, slot ripple, mechanical losses (bearings, windage), thermal transients, or drive inverter switching losses. For final design validation, FEA (FEMM, Motor-CAD) is required.
